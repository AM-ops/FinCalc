Interest Rates - Overview

Simple Interest

The interest payments do not earn any interest. The interest is calculated on the original capital amount each time.

Let \(i\) be the interest rate and \(C\) be the initial amount, then the final amount or Accumulated Value (\(AV\)) will be:

\( AV = C + niC = C(1 + in) \)

Effective Interest Rates

The amount of interest earned on a single investment at the end of a time period, shown as a fraction of the initial amount is the effective interest rate. Let \(X\) be the initial investment amount and Y be the amount at the end of the time period. The effective interest rate for the period is:
\( i = \frac{Y - X}{X} \)

Compounded Interest

The interest payments earn interest. Compound interest is the practice of calculating interest periodically and adding it to the existing principal before each subsequent interest calculation is made. This is called capitalised interest. The final amount or Accumulated Value (\(AV\)) will be:
\( AV = C(1 + i)^n \)
where \(n\) is the total number of periods, \(t\) is the number of years and \(p\) is the number of periods in one year, i.e. \(n = tp\).

Notation

In this website, it will be assumed that \(i\) is the effective interest rate per year and \(\frac{i^{(p)}}{p}\) is the effective interest rate per period. This is the proportional increase in an initial investment. Usually the nominal interest rate (indicated with \(i^{(p)}\)) will be quoted as the ruling interest rate and this is quoted implicit per year. This nominal interest rate is then compounded for a number of periods. For example, a nominal interest rate \(i^{(p)}\) that is compounded \(p\) times per year, gives an effective interest rate of \(\frac{i^{(p)}}{p}\) per period for \(p\) periods.

Continuously Compounded Interest

The number of compounding periods \(p\) in a year becomes large, and the interest rate per period small. We will use to indicate continuous interest:
\(i^{(\infty)} = e^{\delta}\)

General Conversion Formula

To convert from one type of interest rate to another the follwing formula is used:
\((1 + i) = (1 + \frac{i^{(p)}}{p})^{p} = e^{\delta}\)
Please also note that interest conversion can also be from one effective period to another effective period:
\((1 + \frac{i^{(p_{1})}}{p_{1}})^{p_{1}} = (1 + \frac{i^{(p_{2})}}{p_{2}})^{p_{2}}\)
where \(p_{1}\) and \(p_{2}\) are different periods.
